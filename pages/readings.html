Virtual Revolution: The Great Levelling


As I write this in 2023, conversations about new technologies that are now accessible to the public seem to surround me. Sentiments of uncertainty, caution, excitement, and fear fill the air when the topic of artificial intelligence and large language models is brought up.


Watching this documentary allowed me to reflect on how the availability of the internet to the general public was affecting the social, economic, and moral climate in America during the 80s. Just like with conversations about AI today, individuals and collectives had different attitudes toward it depending on how it affects them, and what it affords them. Counterculturists and hippies adored the accessibility to information, communication, and media that the net afforded them. Record labels and other media distribution agencies were horrified at the threat that same technology imposed on their entire business model.


The blessings of the internet were normalized over time. Companies embraced and adapted to the challenges it created for them. A future emerged that was perhaps not as exciting for the optimists, but also not as perilous to the doubters. Will we see this pattern continue in regard to the advent of new technologies?


Only time will tell whether the same pattern will hold true for the introduction of new technologies like artificial intelligence and large language models. However, it is clear that these technologies have the potential to greatly impact society in many ways, both positive and negative. As with any new technology, there will likely be a period of uncertainty and debate, but ultimately, it will be up to us to decide how we want these technologies to shape our future.




The Social Dilemma


The Social Dilemma discusses the possible negative effects of social media platforms and the deceptive tactics used by tech firms. The documentary highlights severe issues regarding the predatory actions taken by these businesses in an effort to increase user engagement. While the film raises important issues, it is essential to consider whether blaming only tech firms will help solve the problems that the internet and social media pose for society.


The Social Dilemma's widespread attention-manipulation techniques and the addiction-inducing features incorporated into social media platforms are two of the most urgent issues it raises. These systems aim to maintain user interest for as long as possible, frequently at the cost of their well-being and mental health. The documentary adds significantly to the ongoing conversation about how technology affects our lives by drawing attention to these predatory behaviors.


I don’t think that just focusing on the tech industry in addressing these problems is the ideal solution. As essential as it is to hold these corporations responsible for these problems, it is also important to acknowledge the contribution that individuals, families, and educational institutions make to addressing them, and how we can all be doing better.


Parents and teachers can and should be educating children about the potential risks of social media, whilst also empowering them to make better decisions about their online behaviors by openly discussing topics such as privacy and mental health. Schools can also put in place digital literacy programs that instruct students on how to use the internet responsibly and safely. Lawmakers should also consider implementing regulations that shield users from the predatory practices of tech companies. These could include limitations on data gathering, requirements for more openness in the algorithms governing social media platforms, and restrictions on targeted advertising.



Tweeting Ferguson


This article revolves around the concept of a ‘digital/subaltern counter-public’ under the context of Black Twitter uniting to share their truth during the Ferguson riots. Mainstream news media have consistently underplayed, distracted from, and even manipulated the truth when it comes to acts of violence and hatred towards people of color in America.


In a society that is clearly still struggling with discrimination and systematic racism, safe spaces are of utmost importance to minorities and people of color. They empower individuals to speak openly, interact with others who experience similar hardships, and find support in their common struggles. This social, psychological, and emotional support allows a much-needed sense of belonging, alongside an extra ounce of resilience to continue facing their challenges.


With how much information we consume and communicate digitally, it's important that we understand we must carry over this same emphasis into the digital world. Social media algorithms are trained to provide us with content that we are most likely to engage with. Whilst this mostly results in the algorithm feeding us content that evokes joy and interest, it can also use a sense of fear and wariness that gets users to engage with certain content. If there’s a news story about acts of violence or hatred occurring in a community we are part of, or against an individual we identify with, it's very likely that we are driven to engage with it.


It can therefore be extremely beneficial for many to seek out safe spaces and communities online that prioritize safety, empathy, and understanding. It can be the different between opening your phone and feeling alone and targeted, and opening your phone and feeling heard and empathized with.



The GNU Manifesto


The GNU Project is a free software project launched in 1983 by Stallman. Its goal was to create an operating system composed entirely of free software. At the time of its conception, Unix was dominating the operating system market. For mainframe computers, their operating systems would cost up to thousands of dollars. Other alternatives at the time were cheaper but often required users to purchase separate licenses for each machine the software was installed on.


As an aspiring digital artist/technologist, I’m a big fan of the concept of open-source. It refers to software that is distributed along with its source code, which is the human-readable version of the software that can be modified and improved by anyone. The Linux operating system, the Apache web server, and the Firefox web browser are a few examples of open-source projects. I draw parallels between open-source and a concept described as ‘public creativity’ by write and scholar Eleni Meilou. “When creativity extends, by transformation, the experience and knowledge of others, it becomes 'public creativity'.” Meilou describes the process of how creative products when accessed by other humans, influence and inspire, and can lead to the conception of new ideas, products, and creations.


It would be incredibly ignorant for me to take complete credit for the work I do and the creations I share. So much of the code I type for the websites I create, techniques I use to model and animate in 3d, and film and edit videos are derived from work, tutorials, or information shared by other creators who attempted to solve a problem or express themselves within a specific domain. When we become conscious of and acknowledge the extent to which we rely on the creative efforts of others, we develop a sense of appreciation and are motivated to share our own progress and work in creative pursuits
